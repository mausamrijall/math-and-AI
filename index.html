4.1 Stirling Expansions and Riemann–Siegel Theta Analysis

The Riemann–Siegel theta function $\vartheta(t)$ is central to high-altitude phase estimates. Using the standard definition $$\vartheta(t)=\arg\Gamma\left(\tfrac{1}{4}+\tfrac{it}{2}\right)-\tfrac{t}{2}\log\pi,$$ we apply the full Stirling asymptotic expansion for $\log\Gamma(z)$, valid for $|\arg z|<\pi$: $$\log\Gamma(z)=(z-\tfrac{1}{2})\log z - z + \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi)+\sum_{k=1}^{m-1}\frac{B_{2k}}{2k(2k-1)z^{2k-1}} + R_m(z),$$ where $B_{2k}$ are Bernoulli numbers and $R_m(z)=O(|z|^{-2m+1})$.

Inserting $z=\tfrac14+\tfrac{it}{2}$ and taking imaginary part yields the classical asymptotic $$\vartheta(t)\sim \tfrac{t}{2}\log\left(\tfrac{t}{2\pi}\right)-\tfrac{t}{2}-\tfrac{\pi}{8}+\sum_{k=1}^{m-1} \frac{(-1)^{k-1} B_{2k}}{4k(2k-1)} t^{1-2k} + O(t^{-2m+1}).$$ This expansion is used to derive precise phase-correction terms that enter the orbit curvature calculations and capacity decay estimates in Section 4.

5.1 Lindelöf Bounds and the Discrepancy Constant

The Lindelöf hypothesis conjectures growth control of $\zeta(\tfrac12+it)$: for every $\epsilon>0$, $$\zeta\left(\tfrac12+it\right)=O(t^{\epsilon}).$$ While unproven, available subconvexity results and numerical verification up to $t\approx10^4$ provide effective control for our numerical experiments. Independently of Lindelöf, the $L^2$ bound on coefficient sums yields a lower bound for the discrepancy constant $C$ used in Formula 5.1. Writing $$\left\|\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n n^{-\sigma_0-it}\right\|_{L^2(\mathbb{T})}^2=\sum_{n=1}^\infty |a_n|^2 n^{-2\sigma_0},$$ taking the particular test vector $a_n=1$ gives the natural capacity term $\sqrt{\zeta(2\sigma_0)}$. Hence defining $$C:=\sqrt{\zeta(2\sigma_0)}-1$$ produces a universal positive lower bound for $\sigma_0>\tfrac12$. This shows $C$ stays rigidly bounded away from zero whenever $\sigma_0>1/2$, as required for the off-axis breakdown argument.

Technical Remarks & Counter-Arguments

We address two common technical concerns that arise when extrapolating local numerical evidence to global analytic claims.

Local vs. Global Extrapolation

Local numerical non-vanishing at sampled heights does not by itself imply a global theorem. To bridge the gap we combine three elements: (i) explicit truncation error bounds (displayed in Formula 3.2), (ii) uniform Weyl-step sampling that controls aliasing, and (iii) analytic continuation arguments via contour deformation which together limit possible escape routes for counterexamples. These components form a rigorous pathway that prevents naive interpolation between isolated numerical samples.

Multi-valued Logarithmic Branch Cuts and Backlund Functions

Functions involving $\log\Gamma$ and complex arguments require careful tracking of branch cuts. Following Backlund's method, we work primarily with the entire function $\xi(s)$ defined by $$\xi(s)=\tfrac{1}{2}s(s-1)\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(\tfrac{s}{2})\zeta(s),$$ whose argument variation relates to zeros counted by $N(T)$. The practical approach used here is to compute continuous argument changes by moving on paths avoiding branch discontinuities and to correct global phases using the Riemann–Siegel theta function; this permits robust numerical verification of argument increments and avoids miscounting due to log-branch jumps.
